# Chapter 7: Evolution ## Darwin's Theory of Evolution **Evolution** is the change in the heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. **Charles Darwin's** theory of evolution by natural selection is the foundation of modern evolutionary theory. Darwin's theory is based on four key observations: 1. **Overproduction:** Organisms produce more offspring than can survive. 2. **Variation:** There is variation among individuals within a population. 3. **Competition:** Organisms compete for limited resources. 4. **Survival of the Fittest:** Individuals with traits that are best suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. ## Evidence for Evolution There is a vast amount of evidence that supports the theory of evolution, including: * **The fossil record:** Fossils show that life on Earth has changed over time. * **Comparative anatomy:** Homologous structures (structures that are similar in different species because they were inherited from a common ancestor) and vestigial structures (structures that have no apparent function but are residual parts from a past ancestor) provide evidence of common ancestry. * **Comparative embryology:** The early stages of development of different species are very similar, which suggests that they share a common ancestor. * **Biogeography:** The geographic distribution of species provides evidence of evolution. * **Molecular biology:** Similarities in DNA and protein sequences between different species provide evidence of common ancestry. ## Mechanisms of Evolution Natural selection is not the only mechanism of evolution. Other mechanisms include: * **Genetic drift:** Random changes in the allele frequencies of a population. * **Gene flow:** The transfer of alleles from one population to another. * **Mutation:** A change in the DNA sequence of an organism. ## Speciation **Speciation** is the evolutionary process by which new biological species arise. Speciation can occur in two main ways: * **Allopatric speciation:** A new species forms when a population is geographically isolated from the parent population. * **Sympatric speciation:** A new species forms without geographic isolation.
