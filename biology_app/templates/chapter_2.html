# Chapter 2: The Chemical Basis of Life ## Atoms, Elements, and Compounds All matter, including living organisms, is composed of **atoms**, the smallest unit of an element that retains the properties of that element. An **element** is a pure substance that consists entirely of one type of atom. The most common elements in living organisms are carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), and nitrogen (N). A **compound** is a substance formed by the chemical combination of two or more elements in definite proportions. For example, water (Hâ‚‚O) is a compound made of two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom. ## Chemical Bonds Atoms in compounds are held together by **chemical bonds**. There are two main types of chemical bonds: * **Covalent bonds:** Formed when electrons are shared between atoms. * **Ionic bonds:** Formed when one or more electrons are transferred from one atom to another. ## Water and its Properties Water is a polar molecule, meaning it has a slight positive charge on one end and a slight negative charge on the other. This polarity allows water to form hydrogen bonds with other water molecules, which gives it several unique properties that are essential for life: * **Cohesion and Adhesion:** Water molecules stick to each other (cohesion) and to other substances (adhesion). * **High Specific Heat:** Water can absorb a large amount of heat before its temperature changes significantly. * **High Heat of Vaporization:** Water requires a large amount of heat to evaporate. * **Universal Solvent:** Water can dissolve a wide variety of substances. * **Density of Ice:** Solid water (ice) is less dense than liquid water, which is why ice floats. ## Macromolecules: Carbohydrates, Lipids, Proteins, Nucleic Acids Living organisms are made up of large, complex molecules called **macromolecules**. There are four main types of macromolecules: * **Carbohydrates:** Made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, carbohydrates are the main source of energy for living things. They include simple sugars like glucose and complex carbohydrates like starch and cellulose. * **Lipids:** Made mostly of carbon and hydrogen, lipids are used to store energy, form cell membranes, and act as chemical messengers. They include fats, oils, and waxes. * **Proteins:** Made of amino acids, proteins have a wide variety of functions, including acting as enzymes, providing structural support, and transporting substances. * **Nucleic Acids:** Made of nucleotides, nucleic acids store and transmit genetic information. The two types of nucleic acids are deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA).
